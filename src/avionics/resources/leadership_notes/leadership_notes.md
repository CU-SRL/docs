### Starting Out
The first time sending an announcement or calling a meeting is always scary. You wonder if anyone will even show up, or if they do if they'll be willing to follow your vision for where the team should go. That is completely normal, and it is a good sign if you feel this way.

To be honest, in the beginning this is completely valid. Leadership transitions can be very difficult on a team. You may lose some core members, and progress may slow for a little bit as you learn the ropes. You will need to work hard to attempt to mitigate this as much as possible, but also accept that it is natural.

Do not expect to perfectly fit into your role in the first semester, and even sometimes in the first year. It takes a while to learn your team well and get an instinct for the way SRL operates internally. Over time it will start to feel like a much better fit, and hopefully you will personally identify with the role. Doing so will help you take pride in avionics' growth and capabilities. Personally it took me over a year to feel comfortable making executive decisions, and I often still have a hard time with it. 

### Personal Leadership

As a leader, it is incredibly important to hold yourself to a high standard. This is for a number of reasons:

1. **You are the face of your subteam to the rest of the world.** CU SRL is recognized as one of the most advanced rocket clubs in the country, and its leads need to act accordingly, showing that we are a professional, competent, and mature team.
    - You will also be responsible for meeting with prospective sponsors. In these meetings, it is your job to show them that your team is able to achieve incredible things, and their belief in that starts as early as their first impression of you. 
2. **As a lead, you shape the culture of the team you are responsible for.** If you act without self-discipline (not following through on commitments, breaking university rules without consideration for the potential consequences for your team, etc.), you signal to your team that that type of behavior is acceptable, and that they are not your main priority.
    - This is one of the main ways that technical leaders earn respect. You can never expect to be the smartest person in the room in every area of engineering, but your team will respect you if they can see you are willing to put your all into the team including acting in a way that dignifies the role.
    - Everyone in the club is very busy with classes and other commitments. If they don't think you are committed, there is no reason for them to commit. But if they see that the team you lead is your top priority, they will know that they will not be wasting their time if they put it towards the team.

Specific standards that I'm referencing:
- Have equal respect for all your teammates, whether they are new members or seasoned veterans. 
- Do not permit any form of joke at another's expense (unless the people in question are good friends outside of the club). Feel comfortable calling people out on this calmly but firmly, to ensure that all new members feel welcome.
- Embody the motto that "leaders get to take none of the credit and all of the blame." When something goes right, celebrate your team and the hard work they have done. When something goes wrong, look inwards and ask yourself how you could've led your team differently to a different outcome. 

One specific standard I want to emphasize above the others: 

**HONOR YOUR COMMITMENTS, AND DO NOT OVERCOMMIT YOURSELF.**

As engineers, we tend to be a bit scatterbrained and want to do whatever looks interesting to us. However, if you pull yourself in too many directions as a lead you can do a lot of damage to your team. You will become a bottleneck for everything you've involved yourself in, which will prevent your engineers from completing their goals. 

In addition, do not promise to do anything that you know you do not have time for. If you promise to do something but don't follow through repeatedly, you will destroy your credibility with your team. 

This is why it is incredibly important to know yourself well and have strong self discipline before taking on a leadership role.

### Infrastructure

The infrastructure a team has or doesn't have is, in my opinion, the difference between a world-class organization and a dying club. Infrastructure as I'm using it here covers a wide umbrella of things:

- Lab/work space
- Common work times
- Formalized project development process and pipeline
- DOCUMENTATION DOCUMENTATION DOCUMENTATION
    - Heritage designs
    - Software examples for common applications
    - Recordings of designs for posterity
    - Software installation instructions
- Connections to industry for mentorship
- Lab equipment/resources
- Institutional knowledge
- Remote access/card access
- Unit testing and simulation frameworks

As the lead, building this infrastructure is your main project. If you can build these things, you will build a strong organization that will have a much easier time moving past obstacles and will face less obstacles in the first place.
